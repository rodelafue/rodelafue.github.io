Research

My research interests integrate techniques from different fields. Instead of falling on the fallacy of “not invented here”, I take advantage of technology that has been already invented somewhere else and I try to make it work with concepts in various industrial engineering environments, most specifically simulation and operations. I have always been intrigued with how algorithms work and how I could improve them. My current research is a blend of different topics because, from my perspective, there is no "silver bullet" to tackle complex large-scale problems. Thereby, I resort in mixing field of knowledge to solve the problem at hand. Below I have organized some of my research projects in four general topics.

Research Projects

Ride-Hailing policies for the city of San Francisco

In this work we explore the ride-hailing assignment problem for the city of San Francisco, USA. Our aim was to optimize drivers' behaviors with respect to simultaneous objectives such as maximizing service level, minimizing CO2 emissions, and minimizing riders' waiting times. We tested four different policies with a real-world case study which involved GIS and GPS data. We developed, in Python, a realistic simulation model that captured the most relevant characteristics of a ride-hailing system; moreover, we used the actual road networks to approximate real-time road conditions. Furthermore, it is the first work that tests the effects of different passengers' arrival conditions and analyzes the multiple objectives for different zones of a large city. Results suggest that different passengers' arrival conditions affect the four proposed policies nearly identically. Additionally, the policy of drivers remaining static (in place), instead of driving while searching for passengers, had the highest service level and lowest average distance per ride. Even though it could sounds unrealistical under current conditions, this behavior could be achieved in the near future when all ride-hailing services are serviced by self-driving cars. This work will be presented at the 2021 Winter Simulation Conference.

Flattening the curve.

During the first days of COVID-19 every media outlet was using the expression 'flattening the curve'. As coronavirus is a socially transimitted disease, the urgency to slow its spread meant that local goverments had to impose restrictive measurements; however, the layman did not necesarily understand why these measures where put in place. The short answer was that slowing the spread had a direct impact on lowering the number of cases requiring ICU beds at once, which meant a reduction on the number of deaths from the virus or other causes. We did this educational project to explain two important points: i) movility patterns have a significant impact in how the disease is spread geographically, and ii) explain that in a Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) model a tall and skinny curve is is bad, and must be flattened if the goal is to save lives.

For the first goal, the video below shows that for the Province of Concepción, Chile, the mobility patterns (from a pre-COVID origin-destination matrix) have an important effect in the geographical spread's intensity, implaying that contiguity is not only governed by physical distances but also by travel patterns. Additionally, if you consider the socio-economic dimension of the problem (the figure above), and efficient handling of the pandemic becomes harder and less intuitive. To model this regional contagion dynamic we implemented a straight forward procedure presented in this Nature article.

For the second objective, we tested three different scenarios: 1) pre-pandemic mobility patters (the same as in objective i), 2) Reduced mobility patterns, computed by expterts, after a curfew and mobility restriction were impose in the region, and 3) the same mobility patterns as in 2, but with early reopenning for all areas when the number of contagion was below a certain threshold. The video below, shows graphically and geographically the effects these policies on "flattening the curve", and the importance of social collaboration to achive the goal of reducing the number of deaths due to the virus.

Real-time ambulance redeployment in NYC

We propose a new survival based real-time optimization framework that maximizes the survival probability of patients suffering from life-threatening emergencies while maintaining a balanced workload among the crew. Our approach embeds a mixed-integer linear programing (MILP) optimization model inside of a discrete event simulation (DES) model (programmed in Python). The DES model, after a predefined number of events, feeds the MILP model with real-time vehicle utilization metrics to maintain an optimal overall relocation policy. Moreover, we evaluate the impact, on the system’s congestion, of a mixed dispatched policy that includes the use of external ride-hailing services for minor emergencies. Our case of study, in New York City, indicates that the proposed model increases the average percentage of life-threatening emergencies responded in less than four minutes in more than 10% when compared to a coverage model from the literature. Besides, when compared to a static version of the proposed model, the online approach improved the number of life-threatening emergencies responded in less than six minutes in 14% overall and in more than 17% for the four minutes threshold. Finally, the introduction of ride hailing services presents an important opportunity for reducing personnel workload by 13.5%, on average, and operational costs in $141,220 per day of operation for the entire system. This work is currently under review at the OMEGA journal.

Food delivery for high-schoolers during COVID-19 times

When COVID-19 hit the Bio-Bio region, Chile, it affected all dimensions of people's daily life. One import challenge that arose when schools switched to online-learning was the handling and delivering of food to students (now at home) with access to meals through federal school meal programs. The regional program office had different ideas about how the delivery process should be, but they wanted to test them to select the most promising approach. We implemented a simulation model that, based on the GIS data they provided us with, evaluated the food delivery process following their proposed strategies and some others we suggested.

Tsunami Evacuation in Antofagasta

Successful tsunami evacuation drills are critical to save lives. However, city-wide site emergency simulations are not only difficult to carry out, but also expensive. In this work we implemented a realistic city-wide agent-based simulation that uses a synthetic population, geographic information systems (GIS), and origin-destination data to study evacuation routes that minimize the overall evacuation time. This topic has a great impact on tsunamis-prone countries, such as Chile, that suffer periodic natural disasters. Our "in-silico" representation of the city of Antofagasta, Chile, also includes street elevations to penalize evacuees'scape velocity. We evaluates four scenarios: i) every family evacuate to a meeting point; ii) an stochastic escape scenario, in which, each family randomly decides either to scape to a building (vertical evacuation) or a meeting point; iii) assign each family to either a meeting point or a building, using an assignment optimization model, and iv) an scenario that study the effect of city-wide mobility (using origin-destination matrices) on the evacuation times.

On the Improvement of Firefighters Response Time

Here we tackled a real-life problem faced by Chilean National Firefighters' Agency. We introduce the Facility Location and Equipment Emplacement Technique with Expected Covering (FLEET-EXC) model, an emergency facility location model that maximizes the coverage of expected demand. We consider multiple regions, demand types, vehicle types, and region-dependent dispatching rules. Our solution approach is mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) model that considers vehicles average utilization to compute expected demand coverage, coupled with an Hypercube Queueing Model who provides the aforementioned utilization. The goal of this procedure is to update parameters until the resulting vehicle utilizations from the optimal solution are the same as the ones used to compute the expected demand of the MILP model. The synergy produced by relocation and location of new facilities notoriously improves the emergency coverage, providing insights for strategic decision making. This work is published in the Computers & Industrial Engineering journal.

Robust Location and Allocation of Firestations

In this project we continued our work with the Chilean National Firefighters' Agency regarding the study of the strategic decision making for proper location of fire stations as well as assignment of vehicles to each station with the goal of improving emergency response. We implemented an iterative simulation–optimization approach that based on some precomputed utilization parameters updates the optimal location of vehicles and fire stations. For the optimization part, we introduce a robust formulation of the Facility Location and Equipment Emplacement Technique with Expected Coverage (Robust FLEET-EXC) model, which maximizes demand considering vehicles’ utilization. Additionally, the emergencies arrival process is modeled by a spatio-temporal sampling method that loosely couples a Kernel Density Estimator and a non-homogeneous non-renewal arrival process with a Markov-Mixture of Erlangs of Common Order model as base process. After experimenting with several configurations we show that the model is statistically better than the deterministic FLEET in all but one of the experiment, resulting in up to 6.42% more coverage. This work is published in the Computers & Industrial Engineering journal.

Tidal Energy Project Evaluation

In a tidal power plant the most important devices are the tidal current turbines that harvest the kinetic energy contained in the tide and convert it into electricity. The number of turbines to install, their spatial configuration, and the connection of submarine cables are critical aspects in the development of marine energy projects. In this project we proposed a mixed-integer programming methodology to design tidal current farms considering both cost and benefits. Our model solves, concurrently, the turbine’s locations and cable infrastructure by computing the number, locations, submarine connections of tidal turbines, and the tidal current farm’s overall capacity. We consider location-based costs, including cable routing and loss from wake effect and electrical transmission, to maximize the project’s economic viability. We demonstrate the methodology’s utility by determining the optimal turbines’ configuration in a real-life case study in the Chacao Chanel, Chile. Finally, we published this work in the Applied Energy journal.

Deep Learning for Wildfire Susceptibility Mapping

Over the last few years wildfires have increased in frequency and intensity all around the world. Wildfire susceptibility mapping with machine learning has been proven useful for fire-prevention plans, turning into an indispensable tool in wildfire prevention. In this project we demonstrate that an Ensemble model, based on two deep learning networks previously presented in literature, can achieved remarkable results for forest fire susceptibility. Our model was trained on data of fire occurrences in two regions in Chile, from 2013 to 2019. Satellite imagery data for fifteen fire influencing factors in the study area were retrieved to build a dataset to extract the samples to train the models. These factors include elevation, aspect, surface roughness, slope, minimum and maximum temperature, wind speed, precipitation, actual evapotranspiration, climatic water deficit, NDVI, land cover type, distance to rivers, distance to roads and distance to urban areas. During training, the best sample patch size was found to be 25 × 25 pixels and the highest area under the curve (AUC) for the Ensemble model was 0.953. The Ensemble model also achieved the best accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, negative predictive value and F1 score. Finally, the predicted susceptibility maps suggest that static variables can be considered as predisposing factors, while dynamic variables affect the intensity of the predicted probabilities, with an important role of the anthropogenic variables. A complete description of this project can be found in Ecological Informatics.

Teaching the Baxter Robot to Sort Obejcts

In this project we taught the Baxter robot to first identify and then sort some elements. My student (Felipe Sepulved) and I experienced first hand that trivial tasks for humans are still significant challenges for machines. For this reason, we implemented two independent object detectors, one per arm, in the Baxter Robot. On its left hand, a shape detector is implemented with data generated using the theory of complex contour analysis. With the resulting data we trained both a Gradient Boosting Classifier and a Multilayer Perceptron Classifier. Now, the right hand, was taught to distinguish colors and the dataset was generated using the back-projection theory, and a K-Means algorithm was used for learning. In our physical experiments Baxter observes a collection of objects, randomly positioned within a search space, with the aim it should be able identify not only the aforementioned space but also recognize each of the objects, deposit them in their corresponding containers, and ignore those that do not possess the properties sought.

The Gradient Boosting Classifier gave the best performance on the left hand of the robot, with an f1-score of 94.65 % over a validation set of 925 images. For the color detector (right hand), a silhouette coefficient of 91.68 % shows that K-Means requires a total of 3 clusters over 654 training images. Moreover, because of the theories behind the data generation process, shape features the features are invariant to rotations, translations, and scaling, while color features are robust to illumination variations. Unknown objects are ignored using properties of the proposed features; thereby there is no need to add more data to account for unobserved classes during the training process.

Machine Learning and Discrete Choice Models

In this project we explore the opportunity of using Machine Learning algorithms to model travel mode choice. Currently, the multinomial logit (MNL) and the mixed multinomial logit (MMNL) model are the most popular algorithms to model the problem. However, machine learning (ML) methods have begun to gain ground in the transportation field, showing a high predictive power that surpasses logit models. In general, studies comparing machine learning methods and logit models mainly focus on predictive accuracy, while others –to a lesser extent– focus on post-hoc explanation analysis. A differencing element of this works was that we shed light on the explanation capability of ML models by computing the effect of different variables not only on the overall prediction but also on the prediction of different choice alternatives using an agnostic-model method. We also present an empirical application using four mode choice datasets where our results show that Neural Networks generally perform better than other models in terms of accuracy and interpretation. Moreover, we emphasize the importance of analyzing the equivalence between the models in order to complement the explanations obtained through the two approaches to support management decision making and to better understand the factors that determine people’s travel behavior.

Visualization Techniques for Students' Segmentation

This work presents a methodology for the segmentation and visualization of academic information of first– year students enrolled in the college of engineering at the Universidad de Concepción, Chile. By using machine learning techniques and visualization algorithms, we show that students coming from high performance secondary schools have better chances of academic survival during their first year. Besides, they tend to choose either Chemical, Industrial, Civil, or Mechanical Engineering. Conversely, students coming from the worst performing groups, comprised mainly of public schools, enroll in Telecommunications and Materials Science Engineering. We use Self–Organizing Maps to generate a non–linear projection to a two–dimensional space spanned by the students’ academic records. Then, students are clustered applying the k-means algorithm on the resulting projection. Next, we use the Local Interpretable Model–agnostic Explanation (LIME) algorithm to determine the relative contribution of the explanatory variables on each cluster. After that, the clusters are geo–spatially projected to observe patterns among schools and network visualizations are generated to understand the flow dynamics from high–schools to the programs offered by the College of Engineering. Finally, we use the Squarified Treemap Algorithm to analyze the clusters' schools composition over time.

On the Use of Residual Chips for Biomass Energy

This work shows how modern machine learning techniques can be used to solve current problems faced by the forestry industry. More specifically, we compare the predictive performance of several algorithms on estimating the dry weight, in tons, of chip residues. The dataset contains samples obtained during 22 months from 220 trucks coming from 17 different farms located within the area spanned by the Bio-bío and Maule regions, Chile. Once the trucks arrived at each power plant, samples were collected and dried to compute the dry tons carried by each truck, which was set as the dependent variable. Using open-source software implementations of state-of-the-art algorithms it was possible to determine, for our data, that even though the non-parametric models Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM) and Neural Networks (NNET) outperformed the linear regression (LM) model, they are not statistically superior to the LASSO regression (GLMNET), an improved version of the LM model. Additionally, it was observed that seasonality affects the ratio of green tons to dry tons a truck can deliver to a power plant during the year. Finally, the continuous variables green tons, elevation, east and north (longitude-latitude) also contribute to explaining the dependent variable. We published this work in the International Journal of Forest Engineering.

Exploratory Analysis of Fishing Operations

Here we had the challenge of finding a relationship among several oceanographic variables and fish catch sizes that could lead to good fishing zones. We downloaded satellite imagery (from Copernicus) such as seawater velocity and direction, sea surface temperature, plakton concentration, sea height and salinity. With this information we could visualize ocean currents which fishes use as real "highways" during their migration process, being the Humbolt and the Gunther currents the most relevant for our study. With the collected information we also identified places were the sea surface temperature changed abruptly. Finally, using a clustering algorithm we could observe that faster current velocities occur at zones were sea surface temperature change. Interestingly, when we mapped the movement of fishing boats we observed that Captains tend to sail to exactly the same areas where the aforementioned phenomena occurs.

A Syntethic Population for the City of Concepcioon, Chile.

This project is quite interesting because we created a reliable and granular synthetic population to serve as an input for microsimulations of social dynamics. In our case, Chile releases anonymized data, at the 5-zip area code level, of each individual (18 million records approx.), but the contry also provides marginalized information at the block level. Then, the problem can be seen as a particular instance of the generalized assigment problem. Based on this, we implemented a mixed integer programming model that takes advantage of the particularity of the data to generate a synthetic population. The model relies on slack variables to achieve a feasible association between the disaggregated and aggregated data mismatches with the purpose of finding an optimal fit that reproduces the underlying structure of the real population, as close as possible. It does it by assigning households to each of the blocks within a census area (5-zip area code). Likewise, cartographic data was used to generate Lon-Lat coordinates of all households and individuals within their respective blocks. As a case study, the Province of Concepción, Chile, was selected. The results were compared with an heuristic proposed in the literature, showing that our model is superior in fitting the population at the category and area level. Furthermore, the proposed model will finds the optimal solution when consistent and adjusted data are available, but when having mismatched data the assignment will not be optimal but it will remain significant from an statistical viewpoint.

Teaching

My teaching philosophy is a combination of my experiences as a student, a teaching assistant, an instructor, and as a faculty. Throughout the process of obtaining my undergraduate degrees I learned what a good teacher does and does not do. Observing teachers I admired I understood that when standing in front of a class, I have two obligations: 1) present the class according to my knowledge and 2) guide and mentor my students as they learn. Moreover, I do believe that hands-on experience plays a critical role in understanding abstract concepts; then, I make a conscious effort to provide practical examples during class sessions.

Stochastic Models

This course is a gentle introduction, at the graduate level, to stochastic processes. I try to balance the application part using case studies, from the basic and intermediate bibliography, with some formal mathematical theorems and proof from the books on the advance bibliography. Introduction to Machine Learning

This is an elective course for undergraduate engineering students that introduces them to the basics of machine learning. I co-taught this course with Manuel Perez. It starts with a brief review of programming, linear algebra and statistics. Then we move to supervised learning (Regression, Logistic regression, Decision trees, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Support-vector Machines). Next, we study unsupervised learning (k-means, Principal components, Self-organizing maps). Finally, recommender systems are presented as a semi-supervised problem. The main goal of this course is to expose our students to a variety of machine learning algorithms for problems in both industry and academia.

Mentees

As a faculty you have the unique oportunity to talk to an audience, your class, on a weekly basis; however, with this comes great responsibility since not only your teaching philosophy but also your approachability will affect your students' learning outcomes. Moreover, in my experience, interested students tend to stop by during office hours and a more complex mentoring relationship emerges. In this scenario, through guiding and mentoring you convey your philosophy to the student in one-to-one conversations. Personally, I enjoy working in research projects with both undergrad and grad students where in a controlled environment, I gradually push them to give the best of themselves. A crucial point is to learn when to stop exerting pressure on them.

Outreach

I have been always engaged in outreach activities moved by a deep felling of finding a way of giving back to the community for the generous scholarships I have received through my life. I have served as a private in the Chilean army, I have been an ad-honorem lecturer for socially disadvantaged women programs, I have picked garbage from the streets of Philadelphia during a Fulbright enrichment seminar, etc. I learnt that there are many ways to serve the community, but in my personal journey education is one of the most important. Thereby, from 2017 to 2019, in a joint effort with the OZ Cinema (Chile), we offered scientific seminars in a variety of controversial topics with the aim of educating people and combating misinformation.
