Research

My research interests integrate techniques from different fields. Instead of falling on the fallacy of “not invented here”, I take advantage of technology that has been already invented somewhere else and I try to make it work with concepts in various industrial engineering environments, most specifically simulation and operations. I have always been intrigued with how algorithms work and how I could improve them. My current research is a blend of different topics because, from my perspective, there is no "silver bullet" to tackle complex large-scale problems. Thereby, I resort in mixing field of knowledge to solve the problem at hand. Below I have organized some of my research projects in four general topics.

Research Projects

Ride-Hailing policies for the city of San Francisco

In this work we explore the ride-hailing assignment problem for the city of San Francisco, USA. Our aim was to optimize drivers' behaviors with respect to simultaneous objectives such as maximizing service level, minimizing CO2 emissions, and minimizing riders' waiting times. We tested for different policies with a real-world case study which involved GIS and GPS data. We developed, in Python, a realistic simulation model that captured the most relevant characteristics of a ride-hailing system; moreover, we used the actual road networks to approximate real-time road conditions. Furthermore, it is the first work that tests the effects of different passengers' arrival conditions and analyzes the multiple objectives for different zones of a large city. Results suggest that different passengers' arrival conditions affect the four proposed policies nearly identically. Additionally, the policy of drivers remaining static (in place), instead of driving while searching for passengers, had the highest service level and lowest average distance per ride. Even though it could sounds unrealistical under current conditions, this behavior could be achieved in the near future when all ride-hailing services are serviced by self-driving cars. This work will be presented at the 2021 Winter Simulation Conference.

Flattening the curve.

Here we tackled a real-life problem faced by Chilean National Firefighters' Agency. We introduce the Facility Location and Equipment Emplacement Technique model with Expected Covering (FLEET-EXC), an emergency facility location problem that maximizes the coverage of expected demand. This model considers multiple regions, demand types, vehicle types, and region-dependent dispatching rules. This work presents a mixed-integer linear programming model that considers vehicles average utilization to compute expected demand coverage, where a Hypercube Queueing Model is used to compute the aforementioned utilization. The goal of this procedure is to update parameters until the resulting vehicle utilizations from the optimal solution are the same as the ones used to compute the expected demand of the MIP mentioned above model. The synergy produced by relocation and location of new facilities notoriously improves the emergency coverage, providing insights for strategic decision making. This work ended up being published in the in this Nature article we could imprement a straight forward regional contagion dynamic.

Online ambulances redeployment in NYC

In this work we propose a new survival based real-time optimization framework that maximizes the survival probability of patients suffering from life-threatening emergencies while maintaining a balanced workload among the crew. Our approach implements an optimization model inside of a discrete event simulation model (implement in Python) that, after a predefined number of events, feeds the former with real-time vehicle utilization metrics to maintain an optimal overall relocation policy. Moreover, we evaluate the impact, on the system’s congestion, of a mixed dispatched policy that includes the use of external ride-hailing services for minor emergencies. Our case of study, in New York City, indicates that the proposed model increases the average percentage of life-threatening emergencies responded in less than four minutes in more than 10% when compared to a coverage model from the literature. Besides, when compared to a static version of the proposed model, the online approach improved the number of life-threatening emergencies responded in less than six minutes in 14% overall and in more than 17% for the four minutes threshold. Finally, the introduction of ride hailing services presents an important opportunity for reducing personnel workload by 13.5%, on average, and operational costs in $141,220 per day of operation for the entire system. This work is currently under review at the OMEGA journal.

Food delivery for high-schoolers during COVID times

When COVID hit the Bio-Bio region, Chile, it affected all dimensions of people's daily life. One import challenge that arose when schools switched to online-learning was how to deliver food to students (now at home) with access to meals through federal school meal programs. The manager in charge of the regional program had different ideas about how the delivery process could be, but they wanted to test them to select the most promising approach. We implemented a simulation model that, based on the GIS data they provided us with, evaluated the food delivery process following their proposed strategies and some others we suggested.

Tsunami Evacuation in Antofagasta

Successful tsunami evacuation drills are critical to save lives. However, city-wide disaster preparedness drill are not only difficult to carry out, but also expensive. In this work we implemented a realistic city-wide agent-based simulation that uses a synthetic population, GIS data, and origin-destination data to study evacuation routes that minimizes the overall evacuation time. This topic has a great impact on tsunamis-prone countries, such as Chile, that suffer periodic natural disasters. Our "in-silico" representation of the city of Antofagasta, Chile, also includes street elevations to penalize evacuees' velocity. We evaluates four scenarios: i) every family evacuate to a meeting point; ii) an stochastic escape scenario, in which, each family stochastically decides either to scape to a building or a meeting point; iii) Assign each family to either a meeting point or a building, using an assignment optimization model, and iv) an scenario that study the effect of city-wide mobility (using origin-destination matrices) on the evacuation times.

Robust Location and Allocation of Firestations

In this project we continued our work with the Chilean National Firefighters' Agency regarding the study of the strategic decision making regarding the proper location of fire stations as well as their assignment of vehicles to improve emergency response. We implemented an iterative simulation–optimization approach that based on some precomputed utilization parameters updates the optimal location of vehicles and fire stations. For the optimization part, we introduce a robust formulation of the Facility Location and Equipment Emplacement Technique with Expected Coverage (Robust FLEET-EXC) model, which maximizes demand considering vehicles’ utilization. Additionally, the emergencies arrival process is modeled by a spatio-temporal sampling method that loosely couples a Kernel Density Estimator and a non-homogeneous non-renewal arrival process with a Markov-Mixture of Erlangs of Common Order model as base process. After experimenting with several configurations we show that the model is statistically better than the deterministic FLEET in all but one of the experiment, resulting in up to 6.42% more coverage. The academic part of this work is published in the Computers & Industrial Engineering journal.

Tidal Energy Project Evaluation

In a tidal power plant the most important devices are the tidal current turbines that harvest the kinetic energy contained in the tide and convert it into electricity. The number of turbines to install, their spatial configuration, and the connection of submarine cables are critical aspects in the development of marine energy projects. In this project we proposed a mixed-integer programming methodology to design tidal current farms considering both cost and benefits. Our model solves, concurrently, the turbine’s locations and cable infrastructure by computing the number, locations, submarine connections of tidal turbines, and the tidal current farm’s overall capacity. We consider location-based costs, including cable routing and loss from wake effect and electrical transmission, to maximize the project’s economic viability. We demonstrate the methodology’s utility by determining the optimal turbines’ configuration in a real-life case study in the Chacao Chanel, Chile. Finally, we published this work in the Applied Energy journal.

Deep Learning for Wildfire Susceptibility Mapping

Over the last few years wildfires have increased in frequency and intensity all around the world. Wildfire susceptibility mapping with machine learning has been proven useful for fire-prevention plans, turning into an indispensable tool in wildfire prevention. In this project we demonstrate that an Ensemble model, based on two deep learning networks previously presented in literature, can achieved remarkable results for forest fire susceptibility. Our model was trained on data of fire occurrences in two regions in Chile, from 2013 to 2019. Satellite imagery data for fifteen fire influencing factors in the study area were retrieved to build a dataset to extract the samples to train the models. These factors include elevation, aspect, surface roughness, slope, minimum and maximum temperature, wind speed, precipitation, actual evapotranspiration, climatic water deficit, NDVI, land cover type, distance to rivers, distance to roads and distance to urban areas. During training, the best sample patch size was found to be 25 × 25 pixels and the highest area under the curve (AUC) for the Ensemble model was 0.953. The Ensemble model also achieved the best accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, negative predictive value and F1 score. Finally, the predicted susceptibility maps suggest that static variables can be considered as predisposing factors, while dynamic variables affect the intensity of the predicted probabilities, with an important role of the anthropogenic variables. A complete description of this project can be found in Ecological Informatics.

Teaching the Baxter Robot to Sort Obejcts

In this project we taught the Baxter robot to first identify and then sort some elements. My student (Felipe Sepulved) and I experienced first hand that trivial tasks for humans are significant challenges for machines. For this reason, we implemented two independent object detectors, one per arm, in the Baxter Robot. On its left hand, a shape detector is implemented with data generated using the theory of complex contour analysis. With the data we trained both a Gradient Boosting Classifier and a Multilayer Perceptron Classifier. Now, the right hand, was taught to distinguish colors and the dataset was generated using the back-projection theory, and a K-Means algorithm was used for learning. In our experiments Baxter faces a collection of objects randomly positioned within a search space with the aim it should be able identify the aforementioned space, recognize each of the objects, deposit them in their corresponding containers, and ignore those that do not possess the properties sought. For the left hand, Gradient Boosting Classifier gives the best performance, with an f1-score of 94.65 % over a validation set of 925 images. For the color detector, the silhouette coefficient of 91.68 % shows that K-Means requires a total of 3 clusters for 654 training images. Moreover, because of the theories we used to generate the trainning data, shape features are invariant to rotations, translations, and scaling, while color features are robust to illumination variations. Unknown objects are ignored, using properties of the proposed features, and without the need to add more data to the algorithm training process.

Machine Learning and Discrete Choice Models

In this project we explore the opportunity of using Machine Learning algorithms to model travel mode choice. Currently, the multinomial logit (MNL) and the mixed multinomial logit (MMNL) model are the most popular algorithms to model the problem. However, machine learning (ML) methods have begun to gain ground in the transportation field, showing a high predictive power that surpasses logit models.In general, studies comparing machine learning methods and logit models mainly focus on predictive accuracy, while others –to a lesser extent– focus on post-hoc explanation analysis. A differencing element of this works was that we shed light on explanation capability of ML models by computing the effect of different variables not only on the overall prediction but also on the prediction of different choice alternatives using an agnostic-model method. We also present an empirical application using four mode choice datasets where our results show that Neural Networks generally perform better than other models in terms of accuracy and interpretation. Moreover, we emphasize the importance of analyzing the equivalence between the models in order to complement the explanations obtained through the two approaches not only to support management decision making, but also to better understand the factors that determine people’s travel behavior.

Visualization Techniques for Students' Segmentation

This work presents a methodology for the segmentation and visualization of academic information of first– year students from the college of engineering at the Universidad de Concepción, Chile. By using machine learning techniques and visualization algorithms, we show that students coming from high performance secondary schools have better chances of academic survival during their first year. Besides, they tend to choose either Chemical, Industrial, Civil, or Mechanical Engineering. Conversely, students coming from the worst performing groups, comprised mainly of public schools, enroll in Telecommunications and Materials Science Engineering. In this work we use Self–Organizing Maps to generate a non–linear projection to a two–dimensional space spawned by the students’ academic records. Then, students are clustered applying the k-means algorithm on the resulting projection. Next, we use the Local Interpretable Model–agnostic Explanation algorithm to determine the relative contribution of the explanatory variables in each subset of samples of each cluster. After that, the clusters are geo–spatially projected to observe patterns among schools and network visualizations are generated to understand the dynamics of how high–schools students flow to the programs offered by the College of Engineering. Finally, we use the Squarified Treemap Algorithm to analyze the composition of schools in each cluster over time.

On the Use of Residual Chips for Biomass Energy

This work shows how modern machine learning techniques can be used to solve current problems faced by the forestry industry. More specifically, the focus is on comparing the predictive performance of several algorithms on estimating the dry weight, in tons, of chip residues. The dataset contains samples obtained during 22 months from 220 trucks coming from 17 different farms located within the area spanned by the Bio-bío and Maule regions, Chile. Once the trucks arrived, samples were collected and dried to compute the dry tons carried by each truck, which was set as the dependent variable. Using open-source software implementations of state-of-the-art algorithms it was possible to determine, for our data, that even though the non-parametric models Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM) and Neural Networks (NNET) outperformed the linear regression (LM) model, they are not statistically superior to the LASSO regression (GLMNET), an improved version of the LM model. Additionally, it was observed that seasonality affects the ratio of green tons to dry tons a truck can deliver to a power plant during the year. Finally, the continuous variables green tons, elevation, east and north (longitude-latitude) also contribute to explaining the dependent variable. We published this work in the International Journal of Forest Engineering

Exploratory Analysis of Fishing Operations

Here we had the challenge to find a relationship among several oceanographic variables and fish catch sizes in order to find good fishing zones. We downloaded satellite imagery (from Copernicus) regarding seawater velocity and direction, sea surface temperature, plakton concentration, sea height and salinity. With this information we could visualize ocean currents which fishes use as real "highways". For our case, the Humbolt and the Gunther currents were the important ones to study. We could also identify places were the sea surface temperature changed abruptly. Finally, using a clustering algorithm we could identify that larger current velocities occur at on zones were sea surface temperature change. Interestingly, when we mapped the movement of fishing boats we observed that Captains sail to exactly the same areas we found in our analysis.

A Syntethic Population for the City of Concepcioon, Chile.

This project is quite interesting because we created a reliable and granular synthetic population to serve as an input for microsimulations of social dynamics. In our case, Chile releases anonimazed data, at the 5-zip area code level, of each individual (18 million records approx.), but the contry also provides marginalized information at the block level. Then, the problem can be seen as a particular instance of the generalized assigment problem. Based on this, we implemented a mixed integer programming model that takes advantage of the particularity of the data. The model relies on slack variables to achieve a feasible association between the disaggregated and aggregated data mismatches, and its purpose is to achieve an optimal fit that allows reproducing the underlying structure of the real population, as close as possible, by assigning households to each of the blocks within a census area (5-zip area code). Likewise, cartographic data was used to generate a Lon-Lat coordinates of all households and individuals within their respective blocks. As a case study, the province of Concepcion in Chile was selected. The results of the model were compared with a heuristic similar to those proposed in the literature, showing that the model is superior in fitting the population at the category and area level. Furthermore, we showed that the proposed model finds the optimal solution when consistent and adjusted data are available, and that despite having mismatched data, the assignment remains significant from an statistical viewpoint.
