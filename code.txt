															<h2 class="mb-4">On the Use of Residual Chips for Biomass Energy</h2>
															<div class="col-md-11">
																<center>
																<img src="images/Research/MachineLearning/ChipsTerrain.PNG" width="400" height="121" alt="" class="img-fluid">
																</center>
																<p>This work shows how modern machine learning techniques can be used to solve current problems faced by 
																	the forestry industry. More specifically, the focus is on comparing the predictive performance of several 
																	algorithms on estimating the dry weight, in tons, of chip residues. The dataset contains samples obtained 
																	during 22 months from 220 trucks coming from 17 different farms located within the area spanned by the 
																	Bio-bío and Maule regions, Chile. Once the trucks arrived, samples were collected and dried to compute the 
																	dry tons carried by each truck, which was set as the dependent variable. Using open-source software 
																	implementations of state-of-the-art algorithms it was possible to determine, for our data, that even though 
																	the non-parametric models Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM) and Neural Networks (NNET) outperformed 
																	the linear regression (LM) model, they are not statistically superior to the LASSO regression (GLMNET), an 
																	improved version of the LM model. Additionally, it was observed that seasonality affects the ratio of green 
																	tons to dry tons a truck can deliver to a power plant during the year. Finally, the continuous variables green 
																	tons, elevation, east and north (longitude-latitude) also contribute to explaining the dependent variable. We published
																	this work in the <a href="https://doi.org/10.1080/14942119.2021.1892415">International Journal of Forest Engineering</a>
																</p>

																<p>
																Here we had the challenge to find a relationship among several oceanographic variables
																and fish catch sizes in order to find good fishing zones. We downloaded satellite imagery (from <a href="https://www.copernicus.eu/en">Copernicus</a>)
																regarding seawater velocity and direction, sea surface temperature, plakton concentration, sea height and salinity. 
																With this information we could visualize ocean currents which fishes use as real "highways".
																For our case, the Humbolt and the Gunther currents were the important ones to study. We could also
																identify places were the sea surface temperature changed abruptly. Finally, using a clustering algorithm
																we could identify that larger current velocities occur at on zones were sea surface temperature change. 
																Interestingly, when we mapped the movement of fishing boats we observed that Captains sail to exactly
																the same areas we found in our analysis.

															<h2 class="mb-4">A Syntethic Population for the City of Concepcioon, Chile.</h2>
															<div class="col-md-11">
																<center>
																<img src="images/Research/GIS/SyntheticPopulation/descripcion_problema1.png" width="400" height="121" alt="" class="img-fluid">
																</center>
																<p> This project is quite interesting because we created a reliable and granular synthetic population to serve as an input for
																	microsimulations of social dynamics. In our case, Chile releases anonimazed data, at the 5-zip area code level, of each individual (18 million records approx.),
																	but the contry also provides marginalized information at the block level. Then, the problem can be seen as a particular instance of the
																	generalized assigment problem. Based on this, we implemented a mixed integer programming model 
																	that takes advantage of the particularity of the
																	data. The
																	model relies on slack variables to achieve a feasible association between the disaggregated and
																	aggregated data mismatches, and its purpose is to achieve an optimal fit that allows reproducing
																	the underlying structure of the real population, as close as possible, by assigning households to each of the blocks within a census
																	area (5-zip area code). Likewise, cartographic data was used to generate a Lon-Lat coordinates
																	of all households and individuals within their respective blocks. As
																	a case study, the province of Concepcion in Chile was selected. The results of the model were
																	compared with a heuristic similar to those proposed in the literature, showing that the model is
																	superior in fitting the population at the category and area level. Furthermore, we
																	showed that the proposed model finds the optimal solution when consistent and adjusted data
																	are available, and that despite having mismatched data, the assignment remains significant from an statistical viewpoint.	
																</p>
